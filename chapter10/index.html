    Chapter 10 · Editorial Style

    Good writing is usability.

    A website can have strong visuals and still fail if the words are vague, bloated, hidden, or hard to act on.

    Chapter question: Can users find, understand, and use the content?
    Opening question

    What do people actually read on a web page?

    Titles

    The first promise a page makes.

    Headings

    Landmarks for scanning and deciding.

    Links

    Signposts for where to go next.

    First lines

    The strongest chance to earn attention.

    Most web reading begins as scanning. Editorial style supports that behavior instead of fighting it.
    Useful content

    Content should serve a real need.

    Strong editorial style starts with usefulness: what the user needs, what the organization needs, and where those goals overlap.

    Organization-first

    “Welcome to our website. We are proud to announce our commitment to excellence and community values.”

    User-first

    “Applications close March 14. Start with the checklist, then upload documents before orientation.”

    Writing for engagement

    Engagement is not decoration.

    Relevant

    The content answers a question people came with.

    Targeted

    The language matches the audience’s knowledge and situation.

    Respectful

    The page does not waste time with filler or confusion.

    Prompt: What question did the user bring to this page?
    Cut the filler

    Start with the information.

    Before

    Welcome to our event information page. On this page, you will find important details about the upcoming Night Market and everything you need to know before attending.

    After

    Night Market is Friday from 6–10 PM. Entry is free. Food vendors, student creators, and live music will be in the courtyard.

    Content chunks

    A chunk should answer a complete question.

    Chunking does not mean breaking content into tiny fragments. It means shaping content into useful units that match what people are trying to find or do.

    Chunk test

    Choose a chunk and judge whether it helps someone complete a task.

    Headings

    Headings are promises.

    Headings should help users predict what they will get if they keep reading.
    Plain language

    Clear does not mean childish.

    Plain language reduces effort. It lets more people understand the page the first time they read it.

    Choose a phraseReplace formal wording with familiar wording.
    Inverted pyramid

    Put the answer before the explanation.

    Most important answer
    Useful details and context
    Background, history, extra notes
    Good web writing often starts with the conclusion because users may only read the beginning.
    Prioritize the lead

    Which opening works better?

    Choose the stronger lead.
    Keywords

    Use the words users would search for.

    Keywords belong in titles, headings, first paragraphs, and meaningful links — not repeated awkwardly just to manipulate search engines.

    LinkView parking map
    Descriptive links

    A link should describe its destination.

    Weak link

    For parking details, click here.

    Better link

    View parking and entrance details.

    Link text should make sense out of context because users and screen readers may scan links as a list.
    Visual landmarks

    Composition creates entry points.

    No landmarks

    The event will feature vendors performers parking information schedule details safety notes food options merchandise and other information that visitors may want to review before arriving at the venue.

    Clear landmarks

    When and where

    Friday, 6–10 PM · Courtyard

    What to expect

    Food, art, music, and student creator booths.

    Before arriving

    Check parking, transit, and vendor list.

    Headings, lists, captions, and short sections help both scanners and careful readers.
    Lists

    Lists should be parallel.

    Mixed structure

    • Parking is free
    • Use the south entrance
    • Food vendors
    • Bring cash if possible

    Parallel structure

    • Park for free in Lot B
    • Enter through the south gate
    • Browse food vendors near the stage
    • Bring cash for small booths
    Parallel list items are easier to scan because each item follows the same pattern.
    Structural markup

    Markup says what the content is.

    Only looks structured

    <b>Schedule</b>
    <br>6:00 PM · Doors open

    Software only sees bold text and a line break.

    Actually structured

    <h2>Schedule</h2>
    <ul>
     <li>6:00 PM · Doors open</li>
    </ul>

    Software understands the heading and related list.

    Link visibility

    Links need more than color.

    Readable

    Body links need enough contrast against surrounding text.

    Recognizable

    Underlines remain one of the clearest signals for links in body copy.

    Trackable

    Visited states help users avoid repeating dead-end paths.

    A style guide keeps content consistent.

    Mini style guide

    Voice: clear, direct, friendly · Date format: Friday, March 14 · Button text: verb + noun: View schedule · Avoid: welcome filler, click here, jargon

    Chapter 10 takeaway

    Editorial style is part of the user interface.

    Start useful.Answer the user’s question quickly.
    Write plainly.Reduce effort without reducing meaning.
    Structure content.Use headings, lists, links, and semantic markup.
    Build trust.Accuracy, consistency, and tone affect credibility.
