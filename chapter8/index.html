    Chapter 8 · Graphic Design

    Visual choices should have a job.

    Graphic design is not decoration after the “real work.” It is the planned arrangement of words, images, space, contrast, and pattern so a page feels clear, trustworthy, and useful.

    Chapter question: What should the user notice first, and why?
    Opening discussion

    When a site looks “good,” what are we actually responding to?

    Trust

    Does this feel credible, careful, current, and intentional?

    Clarity

    Can the eye quickly tell what matters and what to do next?

    Mood

    Does the visual tone match the message, audience, and situation?

    Aesthetic judgment happens fast. Design has to earn confidence before the user reads everything.
    Design logic

    A page needs visual interest and useful information.

    Too plain can feel dead. Too flashy can become confusing. The design problem is finding the balance.

    Design is not a skin

    If design arrives after every important decision, it becomes decoration.

    Decoration thinking

    “The pages are already decided. Make it look nice.”

    Design thinking

    “What should the user understand, feel, and do on this page?”

    The flexible web

    The web has no fixed canvas.

    A printed poster has known edges. A web page has many possible screens, settings, zoom levels, and contexts.

    Phone
    Tablet
    Desktop
    Modern graphic design for the web starts with content and adapts outward.

    A page with no hierarchy makes every item compete. A page with strong hierarchy turns content into a guided path.
    Gestalt principles

    People group things before they read every label.

    Design reading

    Proximity: items near each other feel related. Use spacing to create groups.

    Color and contrast

    Color should support meaning, not fight readability.

    Contrast

    Text has to separate clearly from its background, especially on phones and in glare.

    Emphasis

    Strong color is best saved for moments of maximum importance.

    Meaning

    Do not rely on color alone. Add labels, icons, shape, or position.

    OpenClosedSold Out
    A color choice is also a usability choice.
    White space

    White space is not empty. It organizes the page.

    Everything competes

    Clear groupings

    Essential event details appear first.

    Related details are grouped into calm sections.

    Spacing creates order and confidence.

    When everything shouts, nothing is heard.

    Pattern libraries

    A site feels trustworthy when repeated parts behave consistently.

    Buttons

    Primary, secondary, disabled, warning.

    Forms

    Inputs, help text, errors, success messages, and spacing.

    Consistency reduces learning time. Users should not have to re-learn the interface on every page.

    Critique lens

    Graphic design critique should be more than “I like it.”

    1. Priority

    What does the page make most important?

    2. Relationship

    What items feel grouped, separated, repeated, or connected?

    3. Fit

    Does the visual style fit the content, audience, and goal?

    Better critique: “This works because…” or “This confuses the user because…”
