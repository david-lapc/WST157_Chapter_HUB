    Chapter 3 · Information Architecture

    Make information findable.

    A website is more than pages. It is a system of content, labels, pathways, and decisions that help people know where to go next.

    Opening question When a website feels confusing, what usually went wrong?

    Today’s path

    1. Turn messy content into organized content
    2. Choose clear labels and categories
    3. Compare sequences, hierarchies, and webs
    4. Create site diagrams and rough wireframes
    5. Test the organization before building

    Use the arrow keys or the Next button to move through the deck.

    Main idea

    Information architecture is the plan behind the pages.

    Information architecture organizes content so users can form a mental map of the site and move through it with confidence.

    Without IA

    Pages are added as ideas come up. Navigation grows randomly. Users guess where things are.

    With IA

    Content is inventoried, grouped, labeled, diagrammed, and tested before the site is fully designed.

    Good visual design cannot fully rescue a confusing structure.

    Information architecture toolbox

    Five moves turn content into a usable website.

    1InventoryList what content exists and what still needs to be created.
    2GroupCreate categories and use consistent names for them.
    3ChunkBreak large content into smaller, reusable, scannable units.
    4DiagramShow the site structure and page relationships visually.
    5TestCheck whether real users understand where things belong.

    The goal is not to make a pretty sitemap. The goal is to make content easier to find, understand, and maintain.

    Controlled vocabulary

    Clear labels reduce guessing.

    Pick the strongest label for each navigation problem. The best labels match user expectations and stay consistent across the site.

    Choose a label set to reveal the discussion point.

    Chunking

    Break content into meaningful pieces.

    Before

    The event will take place on Saturday from 5 PM to 10 PM and will include food vendors, student artists, music, small business booths, and a community resource table. Visitors should enter through the main courtyard, bring a digital or printed ticket, and use the parking lot behind the building. Some vendors may accept cash only, and guests are encouraged to arrive early because popular food items may sell out.

    After

    WhenSaturday · 5 PM–10 PM
    What to expectFood, artists, music, small businesses, and community resources.
    Before you goBring a ticket, enter through the courtyard, and check vendor payment options.
    ParkingUse the lot behind the building.

    Chunking makes content easier to scan, reuse, rearrange, and turn into page sections or components.

    Site structure

    Different structures create different user paths.

    Structure decision

    Most websites use a mix, but one structure usually leads.

    Sequence

    Best when the user should move through steps in a specific order: lessons, checkout, onboarding, tutorials.

    Hierarchy

    Best when content needs clear categories and subcategories: schools, companies, directories, stores.

    Web

    Best when users explore by association: related articles, tags, recommendations, portfolios, knowledge bases.

    Mixed

    A site might use a hierarchy for navigation, sequences for forms, and web links for related content.

    Group discussion

    What structure would be easiest for a restaurant website? What about an online course?

    Site identity

    A wireframe marks where identity and core orientation elements appear, without deciding final colors, fonts, or imagery.

    A wireframe is not a finished design. It is a conversation about priority, structure, and user paths.

    Testing the architecture

    Can users predict where to go?

    A visitor wants to know whether cash is needed for food vendors. Where should that information be easiest to find?

    Chapter 3 checkpoint

    Information architecture deliverables

    01Content inventory

    A list of planned content, ownership/status, and what belongs in version 1.

    02Navigation labels

    Clear names for the major sections and repeated content types.

    03Site diagram

    A visual map showing pages, categories, and relationships.

    04User task paths

    Examples of how a user moves through the site to complete important tasks.

    05Wireframes

    Rough page layouts showing content priority and navigation placement.

    06Test notes

    Feedback from users about labels, categories, or paths that caused confusion.
